About The Sun


A star around which Earth and the other components of the solar system revolve. It is the dominant body of the system, constituting more than 99% of its entire mass. The Sun is the source of an enormous amount of energy, a portion of which provides Earth with the light and heat necessary to support life.

It is part of the "observable universe", the region of space that humans can actually or theoretically observe with the aid of technology. Unlike the observable universe, the universe is possibly infinite.



Composition


Sun is composed of a combination of plasma, hydrogen, helium, and other gases and metals. It is not a solid mass, but rather a series of layers with different functions. The sun's magnetic field is controlled by a circular electric current, rotating layers, and the sun's ability to conduct electricity.

The sun rotates counterclockwise, taking 25-35 days to complete a single rotation. It orbits clockwise around the center of the Milky Way, 24,000-26,000 light-years away from the galactic center, taking about 225 million to 250 million years to orbit one time. The sun's mass is 5,628 times the mass of Earth. The sun's layers are measured by their percentage of the sun's total radius.


Structure


The sun is made up of six layers: core, radiative zone, transition zone, convective zone, photosphere, chromosphere, and corona.



A. Core

The sun's core, a massive furnace with temperatures exceeding 15.7 million kelvin, is the only location where nuclear fusion reactions can occur. This process, known as a proton-proton chain reaction, releases enormous amounts of energy, far greater than that from a hydrogen bomb explosion. The sun emits photons and neutrinos, which carry and emit the sun's light, heat, and energy, in all directions, continuously.

B. Radiative zone

The radiative zone of the sun, spanning 25-70% of its radius, is where heat from the core cools significantly. This zone is facilitated by thermal radiation, where photons released in the core travel, absorb, and re-absorb for 200,000 years.

C.Transition zone

The tachocline transition zone, created by the sun's differential rotation, is formed between the radiative and convective zones. This region, influenced by the sun's equator's faster rotation, changes rapidly.

D. Convective zone

The convective zone, located around 70% of the sun's radius, is where the sun's heat is transferred through thermal convection through thermal columns, similar to boiling water or hot wax in a lava lamp.

E. Photosphere

The photosphere, the visible surface of the sun, is 400 kilometers thick and has temperatures reaching 6,000K. Thermal columns in the convection zone, resembling boiling oatmeal, are visible in the photosphere. These columns, typically over 1,000 kilometers, last eight to 20 minutes before dissolving and forming new ones. Supergranules can be up to 30,000 kilometers wide and last up to 24 hours.

F. Chromosphere

The pinkish-red chromosphere, 2,000 kilometers thick, is a layer of hot gas that reflects the sun's coolest temperature at 4,400K. Its pink color is due to its temperature increasing with altitude. The chromosphere produces spicules, similar to solar flares, which are 500 kilometers in diameter and last 15 minutes before dissolving.

G. Corona

The corona, the outermost layer of the solar atmosphere, extends millions of kilometers into space. It contains gases burning at one million K and moving at 90 miles per second. Some particles reach 400 km per second, forming the solar wind. Coronal loops and coronal holes near the sun's poles allow faster wind passage.
